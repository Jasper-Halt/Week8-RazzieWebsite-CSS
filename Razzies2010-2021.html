Golden Raspberry Worst Picture Winners

2010 - 2021

An Overview of Every Movie to Win the Golden Raspberry Award for "Worst Picture."

Previous Decade: 2000 - 2009

2010: The Last Airbender:
Director M. Night Shyamalan adapts the acclaimed Nickelodeon animated series... by compressing the entire plot of the 20-episode first season into a single 90 minute movie. Lots and lots of narration is used to try to keep the story coherent, as Airbender Aang (Noah Ringer) awakens from a century encased in ice to discover that his people have been wiped out, and that the evil Fire Nation is on the brink of conquering the world. Leaden dialogue and stilted performances abound. Released in theaters in 3D, but the gimmick is so poorly applied that there's no real difference between the 2D and 3D versions.

Strangely Awesome Moment: The final battle ends when Aang summons a tsunami that puts the Fire Nation into full retreat. In contrast to the rest of the film, the music and the visual effects actually work and the image is genuinely impressive. Then the moment ends, and we return to bad dialogue and worse acting.

2011: Jack and Jill:
Adam Sandler as an advertising exec who is visited by his obnoxious twin sister (also Sandler) at the same time that he's trying to woo Al Pacino into doing a Dunkin' Donuts commercial. Haven't seen it, don't intend to. I've never found Sandler funny - and even most of his fans didn't think much of this project.

Strangely Awesome Moment: Thanks to YouTube, I have seen Al Pacino's in-film "Dunkaccino" commerical, which is deliberately bad... and absolutely hilarious, as Pacino does a song-and-dance number while lampooning his own iconic roles. The scene ends with an absolutely perfect comic button. On its own, it would make for a brilliant five-minute sketch - but I'm not about to watch the whole movie just to see it in context.

2012: The Twilight Saga - Breaking Dawn, Part 2:
I've never seen any of the Twilight movies, and I have no intention of changing that. This was the series' final entry, in which Bella Swan (Kristen Stewart) and her vampire lover, Edward (Robert Pattinson) find themselves in the middle of a vampire war after having a baby. Not generally regarded as the worst of the series; I suspect the Razzie was given to represent the entire Twilight saga, rather than just this specific entry.

2013: Movie 43:
Another one I've never seen. A sketch comedy with an impressive cast: Hugh Jackman, Kate Winslet, Emma Stone, Dennis Quaid, Halle Berry, Richard Gere, Elizabeth Banks, Uma Thurman, and many many more. The sketches were shot over several years around the actors' schedules. The end results pleased almost no one. Film critic Richard Roeper dubbed it the Citizen Kane of awful."

2014: Saving Christmas:
I'm not religious, but I have seen some genuinely good faith-based movies. None of those included the name "Kirk Cameron" among their credits. Cameron directs and stars in this vanity project that purports to be a celebration of the true meaning of Christmas. It mainly ends up as a celebration of Cameron's own ego, as he plays himself and spends much of the running time directly lecturing the viewers. Rejected even by its target audience, which Cameron blamed on a conspiracy by atheists. Because the true meaning of Christmas is that it's always someone else's fault.

2015: Fifty Shades of Grey:
2015 saw the Razzies' first tie since 1990. The first of the two, ah, "winners" was this supposedly erotic drama, based on the unintentionally hilarious bestseller. Dakota Johnson plays a 21 year old college student who interviews billionaire Christian Grey (Jamie Dornan) for the campus newspaper. They end up falling in love, and then she discovers Grey's penchant for BDSM. Bad on pretty much every level, though arguably a slight improvement over its source material.

2015: Fantastic Four:
Genius and unlikable prick Reed Richards (Miles Teller) is recruited into a government-sponsored research institute, where he and some sort-of friends build a working teleporter. When they violate orders to use it themselves, rather than wait for experts, an accident gives them incredible powers... which they barely ever actually use. Studio-mandated reshoots resulted in most of the second half being changed; it's probably no coincidence that the second half is where this goes from merely bad to incoherent. Who knew that the cheesy 2005 version with Jessica Alba and Chris Evans would end up being the good one?

2016: Hillary's America - The Secret History of the Democratic Party:
A Dinish D'Souza "documentary." Haven't seen it, don't intend to.

2017: The Emoji Movie:
I also haven't seen this infamously bad animated comedy. The plot centers on Gene (T. J. Miller), an emoji who is an outsider because he has multiple expressions. With help from various other emojis, including the Poop Emoji (Patrick Stewart), he eventually defeats the evil Smiley emoji (Maya Rudolph) and learns to accept himself for who he is. Aww... Yeah, I think I'll continue to skip this.

2018: Holmes & Watson:
Since I find Will Ferrell even less funny than Adam Sandler, this Sherlock Holmes spoof is yet another one I haven't seen. Holmes and his associate, Dr. Watson (John C. Reilly) become involved in a plot to assassinate Queen Victoria. Ralph Fiennes plays Professor Moriarty - casting that might actually work in a proper Holmes film.

2019: Cats:
Les Miserables director Tom Hooper tries to replicate that success by adapting Andrew Lloyd Webber's beloved stage musical. The first problem: Cats barely has a plot, which already makes it pretty hard to adapt as a satisfying movie. The second problem: Hooper attempts to use CGI to transform his admittedly impressive cast into humanoid cats, with results that flew past the uncanny valley before crashing into the Treehouse of Terror.

2020: Absolute Proof:
Another political "documentary," this one by MyPillow CEO Mike Lindell, purporting to prove that the 2020 Presidential Election was stolen. I wish the Razzies would stop giving the award to political documentaries. Bad comedies, dramas, and musicals can be fun to mock; political manifestos are not. Anyway... I didn't watch this, and I don't plan to.

2021: Diana - The Musical:
File under the "What were they thinking?" category. This is a filmed Broadway stage musical about the life of Diana Spencer. There are some genuinely talented people involved; writer/lyricist Joe DiPietro and musician David Bryan previously won Tony Awards for their musical, Memphis. Everything appears to have gone wrong here, however, with critical consensus declaring it an exploitative show that doesn't even offer decent musical numbers. There's not much sadder than a failed, would-be prestige piece.
